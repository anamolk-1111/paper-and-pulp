Raw Material for Paper

üìã Overview
The raw materials for papermaking contain synthetic, mineral or plant fibres. Pulp is the basic and most important raw material for paper.
üå≤ Wood Pulp
Most widely used covering about 90% of paper produced globally. Contains cellulose fibres bonded by lignin.
Softwoods
Examples: Pine, Spruce, Fir | Long fibres ‚Üí Stronger paper
Hardwoods
Examples: Eucalyptus, Birch | Short fibres ‚Üí Smoother paper
üåæ Non-Wood Pulp
Sustainable alternatives used where wood is scarce.
I. Straw or Stalk Fibre
Sources: Reed, bamboo, wheat straw, rice straw, corn stalk, bagasse (sugarcane waste), grass, hemp
Fiber Properties: Short and thin fibers
Composition: Contain lignin, hemicellulose, silica - require cleaning before use
Applications: Cigarette paper, decorative paper, and filter paper
II. Bast Fibre
Sources: Flax, jute, kenaf, hemp, cotton stalk bark, sisal, abaca
Fiber Properties: Long, thin, strong, and pure fibers
Quality: Provide better quality and stability to paper
Applications: Art paper, printing paper, writing paper, cigarette paper, security paper
III. Hull Fibre
Sources: Cotton linters and cotton rags
Composition: Contains very pure cellulose
Fiber Properties: Long, strong, durable, and highly absorbent
Applications: Filter paper, drawing paper, security paper, text paper, waterleaf paper
IV. Recycled Paper Pulp
Source: Made from waste paper or recycled paper
Processing: Ink removed by deinking process using soap or enzymes
Quality: Mixed with virgin pulp to improve quality
Applications: Printing paper and writing paper
‚ö†Ô∏è Not Recyclable: Wax-coated, plastic-coated, or foil-coated papers

Pulping Processes

‚öôÔ∏è Pulping Methods
Pulping separates cellulose fibers from lignin using two main approaches:
üî® Mechanical Pulping
Mechanical energy is used to physically separate fibers without chemicals. Faster but produces lower quality pulp.
üß™ Chemical Pulping
Chemicals are used to dissolve and separate lignin from cellulose fibers. Produces higher quality pulp suitable for most paper types.
I. Kraft (Sulphate) Process
Chemicals used: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) + Sodium sulphate (Na‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ)
Components: Non-organic + Organic compounds
Temperature: 170-180¬∞C
Yield: 45-50% (Most commonly used process)
II. Sulfite Process
Chemicals used: Acetic acid, Calcium & Sodium sulphate (NaHSO‚ÇÉ + NaOH + H‚ÇÇO)
Application: Produces pulp for specialty papers
III. Chemi-Mechanical Pulping
Combines chemical treatment with mechanical separation for improved fiber properties
IV. Solvent Pulping
Uses organic solvents to dissolve lignin, producing high-quality pulp with minimal environmental impact
üìä Process Comparison
Kraft Process: Most versatile, highest yield, industrial standard
Sulfite Process: Specialty applications, good fiber strength
Mechanical: Fast, energy-efficient but lower quality
